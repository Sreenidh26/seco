Food prices are not the only obstacle to achieving food security

Food insecurity in Canada is affected by both economic barriers, such as increasing food prices and stagnant incomes, and systemic barriers like colonialism, racism, and other forms of injustice. Approximately 15.9% of households in Canada experience food insecurity. The concept of food security involves economic, physical, and social access to food, with affordability, income disparity, and discrimination being major obstacles. Affordability is the primary barrier to food access, as food prices in Canada increased by 10.4% in 2022. Income inequality has also risen in the past two decades, and policies like the Canada Emergency Response Benefit have not effectively improved food security for those with low incomes. Indigenous communities face challenges due to historic and ongoing colonialism, which has separated them from their traditional food systems. Discrimination significantly impacts access to food, with Indigenous Peoples, Arab/West Asian, and Black communities being disproportionately affected. Addressing food security requires tackling both economic and systemic barriers. Community-led initiatives, like the Toronto Black Food Sovereignty Plan, offer promising solutions. However, creating long-term change will require significant institutional adjustments by elected officials and industry leaders. Achieving food security necessitates inclusive and just approaches that consider Indigenous knowledge and address racism, colonialism, and other forms of discrimination. It goes beyond saving money at the grocery store and requires a holistic and democratic effort.
