Further food price rises could cause up to 1 million additional deaths in 2023

Food prices in the UK and around the world are at their highest in 15 years, and this trend is expected to continue, leading to potential food inflation in 2023. High fertiliser prices and reduced use may exacerbate the situation, resulting in poorer diets for many people, with up to 1 million additional deaths and 100 million more people experiencing undernourishment. The rise in food prices is influenced by various factors, including disruptions to international trade, higher energy prices, and reduced food exports from Ukraine and Russia. Energy prices directly impact food prices by increasing the costs of agricultural inputs, such as fertilisers, which can lead to lower crop yields and further inflation. Surging energy and fertiliser prices have the greatest impact on food security, and export restrictions play a smaller role in price increases. The projected food price rises could have significant consequences for human nutritional health and the environment, with the poorest populations being hit hardest. Higher food costs may lead to diets becoming less nutritious, particularly for those with lower incomes, resulting in negative health outcomes. Reduced fertiliser use may also increase the demand for agricultural land, leading to deforestation, carbon emissions, and biodiversity loss. While subsidising fertilisers may seem like a solution, it perpetuates an unhealthy and unsustainable food system. Instead, targeted actions to ensure affordable access to healthy and nutritious food for everyone may be more effective in mitigating the negative consequences of higher food prices and transforming the food system towards a healthier and more sustainable future.
