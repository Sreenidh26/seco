Genetically modified crops may be a solution to hunger - why there is scepticism in Africa

>

Africa has been plagued by hunger and undernourishment for years, with the situation escalating in recent times. The region accounted for the highest level of hunger in 2022, with over 340 million Africans experiencing severe food insecurity from 2014 to 2020. Low agricultural productivity and post-harvest losses are among the factors contributing to this crisis. Genetically modified (GM) crops have shown promise in addressing food insecurity, increasing yields, developing disease-resistant crops, and creating varieties that can tolerate drought. However, the adoption of GM crops in Africa faces obstacles due to strict regulations, limited research capacity, and safety concerns. The European Union's stance on GM products and its impact on trade relationships also play a role. A framework called fibre-feed-food (F-3) has been proposed to guide the adoption of GM crops, starting with GM cotton, followed by GM feed for livestock, and eventually GM food for human consumption, ensuring proper risk assessments are conducted. GM technology has already contributed significantly to global food security, with higher yield and production, lower costs, and environmental benefits. Despite the potential benefits, GM crops have not been widely adopted in Africa, and efforts to commercialize them face opposition and challenges. To encourage the uptake of GM crops and create resilient food systems, there is a need to increase investment in research and innovation for agricultural biotechnology, educate and train scientists, involve local scientists in decision-making processes, facilitate information exchange between different levels of government, and promote science communication based on local evidence of benefits and concerns. Aligning policy, research, and science communication can help ensure the safety and positive impact of GM foods in addressing hunger and malnutrition in Africa.
